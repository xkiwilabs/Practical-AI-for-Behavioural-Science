Written Assignment: Popular Science Article with LLM Collaboration

Overview: Write a popular science article (maximum 1400 words) about research that uses machine learning or AI in psychological or cognitive science. This assignment is designed to develop your skills in science communication and in working effectively with LLM assistants.

The Core Requirement: Transparent LLM Collaboration
You are required to use an LLM assistant (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini etc.) throughout this assignment — for literature searching, summarising papers, drafting, editing, and refining your article.
However, you must also demonstrate that you:
  • Critically evaluated everything the LLM produced
  • Verified facts, claims, and citations independently
  • Made deliberate choices about what to keep, change, or reject
  • Transformed the collaboration into work that is genuinely yours
You will submit your complete chat history alongside your article. This is not optional — it is a core part of the assessment.

Article Requirements
Topic: Your article should be based on the same paper you used for your presentation, but you may also:
  • Reference other related research in the field
  • Discuss broader applications of the ML/AI method
  • Connect to other work using similar techniques
Content
Write for an intelligent general audience (e.g., a science-curious friend or a journalist). Your article should:
  • Explain what the researchers were investigating and why it matters
  • Describe the ML/AI method in accessible terms (no jargon without explanation)
  • Present the key findings and what they mean
  • Discuss limitations, open questions, or ethical considerations
  • Be engaging — this is popular science, not an academic paper

Format
  • Length: Maximum 1400 words (excluding references and chat history)
  • Style: Engaging, accessible, suitable for a general audience
  • References: Cite your main paper and any additional sources in APA format
  • Optional: You may include one figure or image (with proper attribution)

Chat History Requirements
Submit the complete, unedited chat history from your LLM interactions for this assignment. This should include:
  • Literature searching and paper discovery
  • Summarising and understanding papers
  • Drafting sections of the article
  • Editing and refining language
  • Any fact-checking or verification conversations

What We're Looking For in Your Chat History
Your chat history should demonstrate:
  • Planning and Problem-solving: You broke the task into steps, asked targeted questions, refined your approach
  • Critical evaluation: You questioned LLM outputs, asked for clarification, pushed back on unclear claims
  • Verification: You checked facts, confirmed citations exist, validated key claims independently
  • Iteration: You refined drafts through multiple rounds, not just copy-paste of first output
  • Ownership: Your voice and judgments shaped the final product — the LLM assisted, you authored

What Will Not Earn Credit
  • Copying and pasting LLM output with minimal modification
  • Accepting claims without verification
  • Single-prompt generation of entire sections
  • No evidence of critical engagement or iteration
  • Chat history that doesn't match the submitted article

Academic Integrity
This assignment requires LLM use — that is not academic misconduct. What matters is:
  • Transparency: Your chat history shows exactly how you used the LLM
  • Verification: You confirmed that information is accurate
  • Ownership: The final work reflects your understanding and voice

What constitutes misconduct:
  • Submitting an edited chat history that hides your actual process
  • Claiming work is yours when it's unmodified LLM output
  • Fabricating a chat history
  • Not submitting a chat history at all

Tips for Success
  1. Start early. Good LLM collaboration takes iteration — you can't do this the night before.
  2. Use the LLM for what it's good at: Finding papers, explaining concepts, suggesting structure, improving clarity.
  3. Verify everything: LLMs hallucinate citations and get facts wrong. Check independently.
  4. Keep your voice: Read LLM drafts aloud. If it doesn't sound like you, rewrite it.
  5. Document your thinking: When you reject or modify LLM suggestions, note why in the chat.
  6. Think of the chat as showing your work: Like showing calculations in maths, your chat shows your reasoning process.
