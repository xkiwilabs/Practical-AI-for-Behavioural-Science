# Week 13 Readings: AI Agents and the Ethics of Modern AI

> These readings support the Week 13 session on agentic systems and the ethics of AI in research, education, and society. Start with the suggested readings — the optional ones go deeper. Everything here is short and readable; none of it assumes a technical background.

---

## Suggested Readings

### 1. Xi et al. (2023) — The rise and potential of LLM-based agents

Xi, Z., Chen, W., Guo, X., He, W., Ding, Y., Hong, B., Zhang, M., Wang, J., Jin, S., Zhou, E., Zheng, R., Fan, X., Wang, X., Xiong, L., Zhou, Y., Wang, W., Jiang, C., Zou, Y., Liu, X., … Gui, T. (2023). *The rise and potential of large language model based agents: A survey* [Preprint]. arXiv. https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2309.07864

**Why read this:** A broad, accessible map of what AI "agents" are — entities that sense, decide, and act — and where the field is heading. Read the introduction and the framework figures; you do not need the technical sections to get the big picture.

**Access:** Free PDF on [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.07864).

---

### 2. van Dis et al. (2023) — ChatGPT: five priorities for research

van Dis, E. A. M., Bollen, J., Zuidema, W., van Rooij, R., & Bockting, C. L. (2023). ChatGPT: Five priorities for research. *Nature*, *614*(7947), 224–226. https://doi.org/10.1038/d41586-023-00288-7

**Why read this:** A short, influential comment laying out the research-integrity questions raised by conversational AI — verification, accountability, transparency, and the need for human oversight. It frames the ethics half of this session.

**Access:** Comment in *Nature* (paywalled), accessible via the Macquarie University library; author copies are also widely available online.

---

### 3. Walters & Wilder (2023) — Fabricated citations from ChatGPT

Walters, W. H., & Wilder, E. I. (2023). Fabrication and errors in the bibliographic citations generated by ChatGPT. *Scientific Reports*, *13*, Article 14045. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-023-41032-5

**Why read this:** Hard evidence for why you must check every AI-generated reference: across 636 citations, 55% of GPT-3.5 and 18% of GPT-4 citations were *fabricated*, and many real ones contained errors. The single most concrete reason to verify.

**Access:** Open access at [*Scientific Reports*](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-023-41032-5).

## Optional / Deeper Readings

### 4. Yao et al. (2023) — ReAct: reasoning and acting in language models

Yao, S., Zhao, J., Yu, D., Du, N., Shafran, I., Narasimhan, K., & Cao, Y. (2023). *ReAct: Synergizing reasoning and acting in language models* [Conference paper]. International Conference on Learning Representations (ICLR). arXiv. https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2210.03629

**Why read this:** The idea behind the "agent loop" in lecture — interleaving reasoning with actions (like searching a database) so the model can check itself and gather information. Skim the first few pages for the concept.

**Access:** Free PDF on [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.03629).

---

### 5. Gelman & Loken (2014) — The garden of forking paths

Gelman, A., & Loken, E. (2014). The statistical crisis in science. *American Scientist*, *102*(6), 460–466. https://doi.org/10.1511/2014.111.460

**Why read this:** Why running many analyses inflates false positives even without deliberate "p-hacking" — exactly the risk when an agent can try thousands of analyses for you. The clearest argument for why automation makes pre-registration matter *more*, not less.

**Access:** Published in *American Scientist*; a free author PDF is available from [Columbia University](https://sites.stat.columbia.edu/gelman/research/unpublished/p_hacking.pdf).

---

### 6. Obermeyer et al. (2019) — Racial bias in a healthcare algorithm

Obermeyer, Z., Powers, B., Vogeli, C., & Mullainathan, S. (2019). Dissecting racial bias in an algorithm used to manage the health of populations. *Science*, *366*(6464), 447–453. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aax2342

**Why read this:** The societal-ethics anchor (and a callback to Week 5): a widely-used algorithm systematically under-served Black patients — not through malice, but through the choice of training label. Bias scales when models do.

**Access:** Paywalled at *Science*, accessible via the Macquarie University library; a free copy is hosted by the [FTC](https://www.ftc.gov/system/files/documents/public_events/1548288/privacycon-2020-ziad_obermeyer.pdf).
