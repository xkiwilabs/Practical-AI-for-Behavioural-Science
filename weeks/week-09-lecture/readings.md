# Week 9 Readings: Neural Networks, Deep Learning, and Their Role in Psychology

> These readings support the Week 9 lecture on neural networks. Start with the suggested readings — the optional ones go deeper on specific topics.

---

## Suggested Readings

### 1. James et al. (2023) — ISLP Chapter 10: Deep Learning

James, G., Witten, D., Hastie, T., Tibshirani, R., & Taylor, J. (2023). *An introduction to statistical learning: With applications in Python*. Springer. https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-031-38747-0

**Why read this:** Chapter 10 covers single-layer and multi-layer neural networks, convolutional networks, and recurrent networks — with accessible explanations and Python examples. Covers the same concepts as lecture in more mathematical detail but still at an introductory level.

**Access:** Free PDF from the authors — [statlearning.com](https://www.statlearning.com/).

---

### 2. LeCun, Bengio, & Hinton (2015) — Deep learning

LeCun, Y., Bengio, Y., & Hinton, G. (2015). Deep learning. *Nature*, *521*(7553), 436–444. https://doi.org/10.1038/nature14539

**Why read this:** The foundational review paper on deep learning, written by the three researchers who won the 2018 Turing Award for their work on neural networks. Accessible overview of how deep learning works and why it has been so successful. The introduction and "Supervised learning" sections are the most relevant for this course.

**Access:** Paywalled via Nature. Author-hosted PDF freely available from [University of Toronto](https://www.cs.toronto.edu/~hinton/absps/NatureDeepReview.pdf).

---

### 3. Saxe, Nelli, & Summerfield (2021) — If deep learning is the answer, what is the question?

Saxe, A. M., Nelli, S., & Summerfield, C. (2021). If deep learning is the answer, what is the question? *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, *22*(1), 55–67. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41583-020-00395-8

**Why read this:** Asks when and how neural networks are useful as models of cognition — directly relevant to psychology students. Discusses the difference between using networks as prediction tools vs as scientific models of how the mind works. The "When is deep learning the answer?" section is particularly relevant.

**Access:** Paywalled via Nature Reviews Neuroscience. Accepted manuscript available from [Oxford University Research Archive](https://ora.ox.ac.uk/objects/uuid:c678b393-ba73-45cf-878b-ef842f725d89).

---

## Optional / Deeper Readings

### 4. Géron (2022) — Hands-on Machine Learning, Chapters 10–11

Géron, A. (2022). *Hands-on machine learning with Scikit-Learn, Keras, and TensorFlow: Concepts, tools, and techniques to build intelligent systems* (3rd ed.). O'Reilly Media.

**Why read this:** Practical, code-focused introduction to building and training neural networks. Chapters 10 (Introduction to ANNs) and 11 (Training Deep Neural Networks) cover the same concepts as lecture with worked Python examples. Uses Keras/TensorFlow rather than PyTorch, but the concepts are identical.

**Access:** Commercial book. Available via O'Reilly online platform — check Macquarie University library for institutional access.

---

### 5. Doerig et al. (2023) — The neuroconnectionist research programme

Doerig, A., Sommers, R. P., Seeliger, K., Richards, B., Ismael, J., Lindsay, G. W., Kording, K. P., Konkle, T., van Gerven, M. A. J., Kriegeskorte, N., & Kietzmann, T. C. (2023). The neuroconnectionist research programme. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, *24*(7), 431–450. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41583-023-00705-w

**Why read this:** Argues for a new research programme connecting deep learning and cognitive science. Discusses how neural networks can serve as models of brain function — when this is legitimate and when it's misleading. Directly relevant to the Think About It prompt about whether "neural network" is a helpful name.

**Access:** Paywalled via Nature Reviews Neuroscience. Free preprint available on [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.03718).

---

### 6. Vieira, Pinaya, & Mechelli (2017) — Using deep learning to investigate neuroimaging correlates of psychiatric disorders

Vieira, S., Pinaya, W. H. L., & Mechelli, A. (2017). Using deep learning to investigate the neuroimaging correlates of psychiatric and neurological disorders: Methods and applications. *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, *74*(Pt A), 58–75. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neubiorev.2017.01.002

**Why read this:** Review of deep learning applications in clinical neuroimaging — classifying psychiatric conditions from brain scans. Shows where neural networks add genuine value over classical methods and where they don't. Relevant to the lecture discussion of when neural networks help with psychological data.

**Access:** Paywalled via Elsevier. Author copy available on [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/312205163).

---

### 7. Lotte et al. (2018) — A review of classification algorithms for EEG-based brain–computer interfaces

Lotte, F., Bougrain, L., Cichocki, A., Clerc, M., Congedo, M., Rakotomamonjy, A., & Yger, F. (2018). A review of classification algorithms for EEG-based brain–computer interfaces: A 10 year update. *Journal of Neural Engineering*, *15*(3), Article 031005. https://doi.org/10.1088/1741-2552/aab2f2

**Why read this:** Directly relevant to the Week 10 EEG dataset. Reviews which algorithms work best for BCI classification — from traditional methods (LDA, SVM) to neural networks. The "Deep learning" section discusses why neural networks don't always outperform classical methods on EEG data, which is exactly what students will discover in the lab.

**Access:** Paywalled via IOP Science. Author-accepted manuscript freely available on [Inria's HAL archive](https://inria.hal.science/hal-01846433).

---

### 8. Schalk et al. (2004) — BCI2000: A general-purpose brain–computer interface system

Schalk, G., McFarland, D. J., Hinterberger, T., Birbaumer, N., & Wolpaw, J. R. (2004). BCI2000: A general-purpose brain–computer interface (BCI) system. *IEEE Transactions on Biomedical Engineering*, *51*(6), 1034–1043. https://doi.org/10.1109/TBME.2004.827072

**Why read this:** The original paper describing the system used to collect the EEG dataset you'll analyse in Week 10. Understanding how the data was collected helps you interpret your classification results. The "Motor Imagery" section is most relevant.

**Access:** Paywalled via IEEE. PDF available on [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/203918281).

---

### 9. Guest & Martin (2023) — On logical inference over brains, behaviour, and artificial neural networks

Guest, O., & Martin, A. E. (2023). On logical inference over brains, behaviour, and artificial neural networks. *Computational Brain & Behavior*, *6*(2), 213–227. https://doi.org/10.1007/s42113-022-00166-x

**Why read this:** Critically examines claims about neural networks as models of the brain. Argues that just because a neural network can reproduce a pattern in behavioural data doesn't mean it explains how the brain produces that pattern. A valuable counterpoint to enthusiastic claims about deep learning in cognitive science.

**Access:** Open access (CC BY 4.0) — free PDF from [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s42113-022-00166-x).

---

### 10. Alammar & Grootendorst (2024) — Hands-on Large Language Models

Alammar, J., & Grootendorst, M. (2024). *Hands-on large language models: Language understanding and generation*. O'Reilly Media.

**Why read this:** Preview for Week 11. The early chapters explain how neural networks learn *representations* — the same concept from this week's lecture, applied to language. Understanding that hidden layers learn meaningful representations is the bridge from neural networks to embeddings and LLMs.

**Access:** Commercial book. Available via O'Reilly online platform — check Macquarie University library. Code repository: [github.com/HandsOnLLM](https://github.com/HandsOnLLM/Hands-On-Large-Language-Models).
